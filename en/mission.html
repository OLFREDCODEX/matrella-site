Mission and research

Exploring the frontiers of social research.

I work on the methodological, technological and organizational changes that shape contemporary social research, with particular attention to evaluation, data quality and the responsible use of digital tools.

Main interests
  • Methodological innovation New tools, combinations of techniques, hybrid research designs and attention to data quality, with a focus on procedural transparency and the robustness of empirical inference.
  • Quality of life of researchers Working conditions, academic trajectories, precarity, organizational well-being and the relationship between professional life, scientific recognition and the sustainability of research work.
  • Uses and impacts of digital technologies Digital transformation, research environments, platforms, AI and information systems as forces that reshape empirical practices, work organization and knowledge production.
Mission

Methodology as a critical space

I approach social research methodology not as a neutral repertoire of techniques, but as a critical space for constructing empirical knowledge. Procedures of data collection and analysis are not only operational tools: they help define what can be observed, classified, interpreted and made socially visible.

My research focuses on the relationships between methodological rigor, digital environments, transformations of scientific work and changing conditions of knowledge production. I am particularly interested in how organizational configurations, institutional constraints and contemporary research logics shape empirical observation and researchers' trajectories.

I consider it necessary to recognize methodology as an autonomous and legitimate space of scientific reflection. Methodological work is too often reduced to an ancillary function or to technical application, while the construction of research objects, analytical categories and validation procedures is a central part of scientific production itself.

From this perspective, methodology is not limited to academic research. It also offers tools for interpreting organizations, information systems, decision-making processes, public policies and contemporary social practices. Methodological reflexivity makes it possible to critically examine how data are produced, ordered, interpreted and used across social contexts.

Giving proper weight to every phase of research, from problem definition to variable construction, from data collection to interpretation, means recognizing that every methodological choice has theoretical, empirical and ethical consequences.

Finally, I consider it essential to build scientific communities grounded in methodological exchange. Empirical research develops not only through competition and specialization, but also through shared spaces of discussion, cooperation and collective reflexivity, capable of making visible the problems, limits and possibilities of contemporary knowledge production.
