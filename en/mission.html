Mission and research

Let them call me
a frontier methodologist.

I explore the boundary between what social research already knows how to do and what it has not yet tried. Testing new tools and new strategies is not a secondary interest: it is part of the research itself.

How I got here

You cannot really know social research methodology before you encounter it.

When people ask me how I became a methodologist, I usually answer that it happened almost by chance. I enrolled in communication sciences with the idea of becoming a radio artistic director. Then I attended M.C. Pitrone's course in social research methodology, and everything changed.

At university, people often arrive with other goals already in mind: becoming a journalist, psychologist, educator. Social research methodology is an unusual subject, often perceived as demanding and distant from one's path. Most students just want to pass the exam and move on. I was instead struck and fascinated by M.C. Pitrone's lectures, and from that moment I kept asking myself how useful it would have been to encounter methodology earlier.

The problem is structural: from childhood we know what journalists, teachers and lawyers are. We see them on television and meet them in everyday life. But the methodologist? The figure has no recognizable face. You discover it only if it crosses your path, and often it does not.

"You cannot really know social research methodology before you encounter it: it remains confined to its own field of use and invisible to those who do not practice it. A real pity." - Alfredo Matrella

A figure to build

The frontier methodologist.

One reason methodology remains invisible is that the figure of the methodologist itself is not clear, neither outside nor often inside academia. Methodology is still too often understood as a technical support for doing research. In reality, it is a fundamental dimension of scientific production, requiring its own autonomous, critical and continuous space of development.

The frontier methodologist is someone who studies and experiments with new research strategies and tools in order to understand their potential and limits.

This perspective implies that every choice carries a view of the world: it defines who or what can be studied, in what terms, and with what resources. Working at the frontier means making these choices explicit, taking responsibility for them and returning them transparently to the scientific community.

Today that frontier increasingly runs through digital environments. Platforms, algorithms, digital traces and artificial intelligence are not only changing research tools: they are changing how we observe, interpret and construct social reality.

Definition

The frontier methodologist

The mission

Making methodology visible where it has not yet arrived.

One of my goals is to help make methodology known beyond the boundaries of academic research. Methodological knowledge can be extremely fruitful in many areas of social life: from work organization to training processes, and from everyday practices to decision-making.

Methodology is useful in social research, in organizations, in everyday life and in training. Not as a technique to be applied, but as a way of dealing with complexity.

Initiative

Homo Methodologicus

To give concrete form to these ideas, I started the Homo Methodologicus project: the attempt to build an autonomous space dedicated to social research methodology, its transformations and its still unexplored possibilities.

In a historical moment marked by rapid digital innovation, it would be useful to have a space where the priority is to do research on methodological innovation itself. A place for experimenting, comparing experiences and keeping curiosity alive about the tools and strategies through which we build knowledge.
